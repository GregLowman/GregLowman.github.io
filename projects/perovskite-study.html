Perovskite Solar Cell
Degradation Study

Eight weeks of experimental measurement, data pipeline engineering, and statistical analysis tracking perovskite solar cell performance degradation over time.

PythonNumPyPandas MatplotlibSciPyScikit-learn h5pyGoogle Colab
View on GitHub

Overview

This was a semester-long quantum engineering lab course project conducted in Spring 2026. Working as part of a three-person team — B Team — we fabricated a perovskite solar cell and tracked its performance degradation over eight weeks of continuous light exposure, measuring current-voltage characteristics and quantum efficiency weekly.

Cell R22 — 8 pixels, 0.14 cm² active area per pixel. Input power: 99.8 mW/cm².

The central engineering question: how does a perovskite solar cell degrade over time, and can that degradation be modeled? My role was to build and own all the programming — from raw data ingestion through final statistical analysis and figures.

My Contribution

My teammates handled physical lab measurements and experimental procedures. I owned all programming work across the full eight-week study:

  • Data collection pipelines — built repeatable Colab notebooks for each measurement week, ingesting raw J-V curve data and EQE spectra across all 8 pixels
  • Uncertainty propagation — implemented full error analysis from three sources: statistical (standard error of repeated current measurements), instrumental (voltage step resolution), and systematic (solar simulator calibration uncertainty)
  • Statistical analysis — weighted linear regression on PCE vs. time per pixel, reduced chi-squared and residual analysis, z-test for two-pixel slope comparison
  • HDF5 data handling — loaded and analyzed LED emission spectra from stressing stations via h5py, including histogram and Gaussian fitting at selected wavelengths
  • Final project analysis — series and shunt resistance characterization over time, sigma clipping for outlier removal, advanced regression, and all summary figures

Baseline Measurements

Weeks 1 and 2 established the initial performance baseline for all 8 pixels before degradation began.

Forward Scan J-V Curves — All Pixels (Week 1)
Current density vs. voltage for all 8 pixels. Two distinct performance clusters are visible — pixels with higher short-circuit current density outperformed others consistently throughout the study.
EQE Scan vs. Wavelength — Baseline
External Quantum Efficiency peaks near 475 nm (~0.95–1.01) and drops sharply at 700 nm, consistent with literature values for perovskite absorbers. Pixel 6 (brown) leads across most of the spectrum.

Degradation Tracking

Fill Factor was tracked weekly across all measurement cycles as the primary performance metric. The plots below show the degradation trend and the resistance characterization that explains the underlying mechanism.

Fill Factor vs. Calendar Week — All C60 Cycles
Clear downward degradation trend across all three cycles with propagated uncertainty error bars. C60-3 degrades most slowly (slope = −1.54×10⁻²), while C60-6 degrades fastest (slope = −3.05×10⁻²). All cycles begin converging by week 13.
Series Resistance vs. Calendar Week
Series resistance increases steadily across all cycles — directly correlated with the fill factor decline above. Rising series resistance is a known degradation mechanism in perovskite cells, indicating increasing internal losses over time.
Shunt Resistance vs. Calendar Week
Shunt resistance drops sharply in early weeks before stabilizing. The rapid initial drop — particularly in C60-6 — suggests early-stage defect formation creating parallel leakage paths through the cell.

Analysis Progression

Weeks 1–2
Baseline Establishment
Initial J-V and EQE measurements across all 8 pixels. EQE peaked near 475 nm (~0.95) consistent with perovskite literature. Pixel 6 identified as highest-performing from first measurement.
Weeks 3–6
Degradation Tracking with Uncertainty Propagation
PCE and EQE measured weekly as cumulative time series with full error bars. Uncertainty propagated from three sources: statistical, instrumental, and systematic. Degradation trend becomes clearly visible by week 6.
Week 7
Regression Analysis
Weighted linear regression applied to PCE vs. time per pixel. Reduced chi-squared (χ²/ν >> 1) and patterned residuals indicated the linear model was inadequate — a decay model would better describe the physics. Two-pixel slope comparison performed via z-test.
Week 8
LED Spectra Analysis
LED emission spectra from all stressing stations loaded from HDF5 files. Histograms and Gaussian fits at selected wavelengths revealed bimodal distributions indicating non-uniform light delivery across stations — with implications for inter-pixel variability.
Final Project
Full Resistance Characterization
Extended the weekly data into series and shunt resistance characterization over time. Applied sigma clipping for outlier removal, advanced regression, and produced all summary figures comparing pixel performance across the full study duration.

Takeaways

This project taught me that rigorous scientific computing is less about the algorithms and more about the methodology — propagating uncertainty correctly, choosing the right statistical model, and knowing when your model is failing. The chi-squared analysis at week 7 was the most valuable result we produced: not because it confirmed the degradation, but because it told us our model was wrong and pointed toward a better one.

Working at the intersection of CS and physical science creates opportunities that neither field produces alone. The ability to write the data pipeline, run the statistics, and interpret the physics in the same sitting was what made this project work as a team — and it's the combination I want to keep building toward in quantum computing R&D.
